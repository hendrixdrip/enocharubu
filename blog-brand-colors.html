How to Choose the Right Colors for Your Brand and Website

By Arubu Enoch  |  March 2025  |  7 min read

← Back to Home

Before a visitor reads a single word on your website, they have already formed an impression. That impression comes largely from color. The palette you choose communicates your brand's personality, sets expectations, and shapes how people feel about your business before they consciously decide anything.

Color is not decoration. It is communication. And like any form of communication, getting it right makes a real difference. Getting it wrong can quietly undermine everything else you have built. In this article I want to break down how to think about color for your brand in a way that is practical and actually useful, not just theory.

Why Color Matters More Than You Think

Studies on consumer behavior consistently show that color influences purchasing decisions, brand recognition, and how trustworthy a business appears. There is a reason major banks tend to use blue. There is a reason fast food chains so often use red and yellow. There is a reason luxury brands lean into black, white, and gold.

These are not arbitrary choices. They reflect an understanding that colors carry emotional and cultural associations that audiences respond to almost automatically. When your brand color aligns with what your audience expects and values, it builds an unconscious sense of trust and familiarity. When it clashes, something feels off even if nobody can explain exactly why.

What Different Colors Tend to Communicate

Before you choose your palette, it helps to understand the general emotional territory of different colors. These are tendencies, not rules, and they can shift depending on shade, context, and combination.

  • Blue tends to communicate trust, reliability, calm, and professionalism. It works well for finance, technology, healthcare, and service businesses.
  • Green is associated with growth, nature, health, and money. Strong for wellness brands, sustainability focused businesses, and anything finance related.
  • Orange and gold carry energy, warmth, creativity, and approachability. Good for creative industries, lifestyle brands, and businesses that want to feel welcoming but still premium.
  • Red suggests urgency, passion, boldness, and action. Powerful for brands that want to feel exciting, but can feel aggressive if overused.
  • Black and dark navy communicate sophistication, luxury, authority, and exclusivity. A go-to for premium and high-end brands.
  • Purple is associated with creativity, wisdom, and mystery. Common in beauty, wellness, and spirituality adjacent brands.
  • White and light neutrals convey cleanliness, simplicity, and space. Often used as a backdrop to let other colors breathe.

How to Build a Simple, Effective Brand Palette

You do not need ten colors. Most strong brand palettes use three to four colors with clear roles:

1. Your Primary Color

This is your dominant color. It shows up on your logo, your main buttons, your headings, and anywhere you want to make a strong brand statement. It should clearly communicate what your brand is about and appeal to the emotional preferences of your target audience.

2. Your Accent Color

This color creates contrast and visual interest. It is often used for calls to action, highlights, and decorative elements. It should complement your primary color without competing with it. A warm accent against a cool primary is a classic and reliable combination.

3. Your Neutral

Neutrals are the background colors that let your primary and accent breathe. Dark neutrals for dark sections, light neutrals for light sections. Off-white, light grey, and dark charcoal are all common choices that keep the design from feeling cluttered.

Practical Tips for Choosing Your Colors

  • Look at competitors in your industry and notice what colors dominate. Then decide whether you want to fit in or deliberately stand out.
  • Think about where your brand will appear: website, social media, print materials, packaging. Make sure your colors work well in all those contexts.
  • Test your colors against white and dark backgrounds to see how they hold up in both light and dark settings.
  • Check that your text color has enough contrast against your background for comfortable reading. Poor contrast is both an accessibility issue and an SEO issue.
  • Limit yourself. Three to four colors is almost always enough. More than that and your brand starts to look inconsistent and unplanned.
  • Trust how the combination makes you feel. Gut instinct, informed by the principles above, is often a reliable guide.

Real Example: This Website

My own portfolio uses a three-color palette. A deep navy blue as the primary, communicating professionalism, trust, and seriousness. A warm gold as the accent, adding energy, creativity, and a sense of quality. And a medium blue as a secondary highlight, adding depth without introducing a completely different emotional note.

Together, they communicate someone who is professional and serious about their craft but also creative and approachable. That was a deliberate choice made to match the kind of clients I want to work with and the kind of work I do.
